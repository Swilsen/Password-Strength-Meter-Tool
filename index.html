Length

Longer passwords are indicative to good password strength.

Aim for longer passwords when able, as each additional character will increase your password strength exponentially.

A password length of 16-20 characters is considered very strong and have a miniscule chance of being hacked.

Complexity

Use lowercase and uppercase letters, digits, and symbols when making passwords.

A varied character set improves password strength by increasing the amount of different characters found in the password.

This makes it harder to guess as the password's constraints are unknown to the attacker.

Uniqueness

Do NOT reuse passwords!

Unknown to you, password leaks might occur to databases hosting some of your passwords.

Repeating old passwords puts your information at risk, as your passwords are now known to have been used previously.

Use unique passwords for each application or device.

Things to Bear in Mind

  • Try to use passphrases when making passwords, with four to five individual words separated by white spaces. It will make the password much harder to crack and a lot easier to remember.


  • Find a nice balance between long and complex passwords. Very secure passwords are ideal, but if you have to remake them every week because they are hard to remember, you will start a tedious and annoying relationship with passwords.

In today's society, online threats evolve as quickly as technology itself. People with malicious intent are more sophisticated than before. It is therefore important to protect yourself online and have a strong password. Two essential tools that help you enhance your protection and add more layers of protection are Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) and Password Managers.

What Is Multifactor Authentication

Multifactor Authentication (MFA) adds an extra layer of protection to ensure that compromising a password is not enough to get authentication to user accounts. It is widely supported and is offered as a free security option.

However, not all sites do offer MFA, but it is strongly recommended to use when possible. After a user has entered the correct username and password, the user will immediately be required to provide more information.

Methods of Confirming Your Identity

  • Authentication using biometrics
  • A prompt on a trusted advice
  • A code sent to a phone or app

What Is a Password Manager and Why Is It Used?

A password Manager is a tool that can help you generate strong and unique passwords while storing them in an encrypted database. eases you from remembering all your passwords. Potentially, all you need to remember is the master password for logging into the password manager.

This has both benefits and drawbacks as it makes it easier for users to not remember all unique passwords for each site, and helps you keep a healthy password hygiene. On the other half it leads to a single point of failure as all passwords are stored in a single database and user.

Brute-Force Attack:

Systematically guesses various password combinations.

These attacks are simple, only requiring spending computational power to launch an attack, but takes a lot of time and cannot be done offline.

Dictionary Attack:

A type of brute-force attack which uses a preprepared dictionary to base the guesses of.

Dictionary attacks threatens the use of unmodified trivial and simple passwords.

Botnets

A possibly vast network of infected computers.

These networks controlled by an attacker allow for many more attempts at guessing passwords, reducing the estimated time it takes for a password to break.

List-Based Attack:

A type of brute-force attack using lists of previously leaked passwords.

Aims to gain an advantage by exploiting previous information about user password hygiene.

Consequences of Poor Password Hygiene ‚ùå

In reality, most data breaches occur due to phishing attacks or similar means by fooling victims into giving up sensitive information. This is due to these attacks being much easier to execute, and on a large enough scale there is a very high success rate.

However, some of the possible consequences of using weak passwords are similar in effect.

Monetary

Accounts taken over by attackers can issue fraudulent purchases, which can be very costly for the victims of such attacks.

Banks and other relevant financial bodies should be informed in the event of a registered password breach in order to freeze funds so that they cannot be transferred.



Psychological

Identity theft is broader in definition, but generally concerns the implications of an attacker gaining unauthorized access to a victim's credentials.

Losing control over accounts and information, or being the victim of stalking or blackmailing can cause feelings of distress and helplessness in the victim, and possibly cause trauma.
